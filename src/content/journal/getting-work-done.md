title: "An Informal Guide to Getting Work Done"
description: "Practical observations on creative productivity, from someone who has tried every system and abandoned most of them."
date: "2023-11-08"
---

I've read the productivity books. I've tried the systems — GTD, Pomodoro, time-blocking, bullet journals, digital kanban boards, the one where you eat a frog every morning. Some of them worked for a while. None of them stuck. What stuck were a few simple observations that I keep coming back to.

## Start Before You're Ready

The biggest enemy of creative work isn't distraction or lack of talent. It's the belief that you need to feel inspired before you begin. Inspiration is a result of working, not a prerequisite for it.

The trick is to make starting as small as possible. Don't open the file and try to design the whole thing. Open the file and set the margins. Choose one typeface. Place one element. The momentum builds from there, and twenty minutes later you're deep in a flow state that never would have arrived if you'd waited for it.

## Protect Your Mornings

Creative work requires a different kind of attention than administrative work. Emails, Slack, and meetings fragment your focus into tiny pieces. Creative work needs uninterrupted blocks.

For most people, the first few hours of the day are when that sustained attention comes easiest. Protecting those hours — even just two of them — for actual making rather than reacting produces a disproportionate amount of your best work.

This doesn't mean ignoring communication. It means batching it. The emails will still be there at 11am, and you'll answer them faster because you won't be context-switching out of deep work to do it.

## Work in Passes, Not Perfection

Trying to make something perfect on the first pass is a recipe for paralysis. Creative work moves faster and turns out better when you work in deliberate layers.

First pass: get the structure right. Don't worry about details, just establish the bones. Second pass: refine the relationships — spacing, hierarchy, rhythm. Third pass: polish the details that make it sing.

Each pass has a different quality of attention. Trying to do all three simultaneously is like trying to edit a sentence while you're still figuring out what you want to say.

## Know When to Stop

There's a point in every project where additional refinement stops improving the work and starts making it worse. The colors get tweaked one too many times. The layout gets adjusted into blandness. The copy gets polished until all the personality is gone.

Learning to recognize that moment — and having the discipline to stop — is one of the hardest and most valuable creative skills. Ship it. Move on. The next project is where you'll apply everything you just learned.

## The System Is Not the Work

Productivity systems are seductive because they feel like progress. Organizing your task list, color-coding your calendar, setting up the perfect Notion workspace — these activities scratch the same itch as actual creative work without requiring you to face the blank page.

The best system is the one you forget about while you're working. If you're spending more time maintaining the system than doing the work, the system is the problem.

Do the work. Everything else is negotiable.
